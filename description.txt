AP2: Data
Our task is to analyze argumentative text (i.e. debates) and determine whether a given argument contains fallacy, and if so, which category of fallacy it falls into. The categories we have considered so far include both logical and rhetorical fallacies. These include personal attacks, such as ad hominem arguments that target an individual rather than their claims, and emotional manipulation, which relies on appeals to pathos like fear or pity instead of reason. We are also looking at appeals to authority that use credentials/popularity, causal errors like false cause or slippery slope reasoning, and generalization errors such as universal claims and anecdotal evidence. We will track misrepresentations, specifically strawman arguments that distort an opponent's position, as well as logical structure errors like circular reasoning, false dilemmas or denying the antecedent. We will also track language ambiguity that obscures meaning through equivocation, amphiboly or composition/division. Finally, we include an "other" category for fallacies that doesn’t fit cleanly into these definitions. 
From this dataset, we chose to narrow down our focus to the subset of data present in "Pros and Cons - A Debater's Handbook" due to the concise nature of the arguments and points presented.
